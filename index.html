About

Understanding Evolution at the Molecular Level

I am a Professor of Biology at New York University and the director of the Gresham Lab. My research lies at the intersection of evolutionary biology, genomics, and systems biology, using budding yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) as a model organism to understand the molecular mechanisms that drive adaptive evolution and phenotypic diversity.

Our lab combines high-throughput experimental evolution, single-cell genomics, and computational approaches to uncover how cells sense and respond to their environment, how mutations arise and spread in populations, and how gene regulatory networks evolve.

I teach Genetics at NYU, where I aim to bring modern genomic thinking into the classroom. Beyond research and teaching, I serve as Vice Dean of the Graduate School of Arts & Science (GSAS) and as the Faculty Liaison for NYU Sydney, supporting NYU's global network of students and scholars.

Research

Key Research Areas

We use yeast as a model to ask fundamental questions about evolution, genomics, and cell biology.

Experimental Evolution

Using continuous culture systems and high-throughput sequencing to observe adaptive evolution in real time at single-nucleotide resolution.

Single-Cell Genomics

Dissecting cell-to-cell heterogeneity in gene expression and revealing how noise and variation contribute to phenotypic diversity.

Gene Regulatory Networks

Mapping how transcriptional and post-transcriptional networks are rewired during adaptation to environmental stress and nutrient limitation.

Nutrient Sensing & Signaling

Investigating how yeast cells detect and respond to changes in nutrient availability, and how these pathways evolve under selection.

Copy Number Variation

Understanding the role of chromosomal aneuploidy and gene amplification as rapid adaptive mechanisms in evolving populations.

Computational Genomics

Developing and applying computational pipelines to analyze large-scale sequencing datasets from evolution experiments and population studies.
