Our Mission

How Do Populations Adapt?

The central question driving the Gresham Lab is: how do populations of cells adapt to new or challenging environments? We approach this question by combining experimental evolution, high-throughput genomics, and quantitative modeling in the model organism Saccharomyces cerevisiae (budding yeast).

Yeast provides a powerful platform — it is genetically tractable, grows rapidly, and can be evolved in the laboratory under precisely controlled conditions. By sequencing evolving populations over hundreds of generations, we can watch evolution happen at single-nucleotide resolution and dissect the molecular mechanisms underlying adaptation.

Our work addresses fundamental questions in evolutionary biology while also illuminating basic cell biology — how cells sense nutrients, regulate gene expression, and manage genome stability.

Focus Areas

Research Programs

Our lab pursues several interconnected research programs that together address the molecular basis of adaptation.

01

Experimental Evolution & Population Genomics

We use chemostat-based continuous culture to maintain yeast populations under defined selective pressures — typically nutrient limitation — for hundreds to thousands of generations. Whole-population and single-clone sequencing reveals the spectrum of adaptive mutations, their dynamics, and their fitness effects.

Chemostat evolution WGS Fitness landscapes
02

Single-Cell Gene Expression & Heterogeneity

Using single-cell RNA sequencing and single-molecule FISH, we investigate how genetically identical cells in the same environment can display distinct gene expression states. We are particularly interested in how this heterogeneity contributes to bet-hedging and adaptive potential in evolving populations.

scRNA-seq smFISH Stochastic expression
03

Nutrient Sensing & the TOR Pathway

The target of rapamycin (TOR) pathway is a central regulator of cell growth and is highly conserved from yeast to humans. We study how TOR integrates nutrient signals to control ribosome biogenesis, autophagy, and global gene expression, and how mutations in this pathway confer competitive advantage.

TOR signaling Nitrogen sensing Ribosome biogenesis
04

Copy Number Variation & Aneuploidy

Chromosomal duplications and gene amplifications are among the most rapid adaptive mechanisms available to cells. We investigate the mechanisms by which copy number variants arise, the trade-offs they impose, and how they are subsequently refined or resolved during continued evolution.

Aneuploidy CNV Genome instability
05

mRNA Regulation & Post-Transcriptional Control

Gene expression is shaped not only by transcription but also by mRNA stability, translation efficiency, and localization. We study how post-transcriptional mechanisms contribute to regulatory evolution and how changes in mRNA metabolism are selected during adaptation.

mRNA decay Translation RNA-seq
06

Computational & Quantitative Approaches

Modern evolutionary genomics generates datasets at extraordinary scale. We develop and apply computational tools — including our open-source R package vivaldi — to analyze viral and microbial variant data, infer selection coefficients, and model evolutionary dynamics.

Experimental Systems

Model Systems & Technologies

We leverage a suite of experimental and computational platforms to address our research questions.

🧫

Budding Yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae)

Our primary model organism. Its short generation time, rich genetic toolkit, fully annotated genome, and conservation of core biological processes with higher eukaryotes make it ideal for studying fundamental questions in evolutionary and cell biology.

⚗️

Chemostat & Turbidostat Culture

Continuous culture devices allow precise control of cell density and growth rate, enabling long-term evolution experiments under well-defined and reproducible selective pressures. We operate both chemostat (nutrient-limited) and turbidostat (density-controlled) configurations.

🧬

Whole-Genome & Single-Cell Sequencing

We use Illumina short-read sequencing for high-coverage population genomics and variant detection, long-read sequencing for resolving structural variants, and single-cell RNA-seq to dissect transcriptional heterogeneity at the level of individual cells.

💻

Open-Source Software

We develop freely available computational tools for the community, including vivaldi, a CRAN-hosted R package for analysis and visualization of viral single-nucleotide variants from Illumina sequencing data, developed in collaboration with the broader Gresham Lab ecosystem.
